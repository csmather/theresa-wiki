French philosopher and practicing psychoanalyst (1964–2017) who built a body of work defending everything a security-obsessed culture files under weakness: risk, gentleness, secrecy, hospitality. In summer 2017 she drowned at Pampelonne beach after going into dangerous water toward two children in trouble (they survived). It's impossible to fully separate that death from the work — she had already written its argument.

## In Praise of Risk (2011)

- Risk is not recklessness; it's openness to being altered. Birth, love, speech, therapy, change — all risks in her sense: you cannot undergo them and remain who you were.
- Her target: the fantasy of "zero risk." A society that promises total security delivers a smaller life, not a safer one — absolute safety is just death arriving politely.
- Clinical resonance: every real step a client takes — leaving, staying, disclosing, hoping — is a risk. Therapy is a practice space for risk, and the clinician's job includes not foreclosing it in the name of safety-thinking.

## Power of Gentleness (2013)

- Gentleness (*douceur*) as an active power, not a failed force — it transforms what force can only break. She traces it through infancy, animals, healing, and its commodification into wellness kitsch.
- For a field routinely condescended to for its "soft skills": here is a rigorous philosopher arguing the soft skills are the strong ones. Rhymes with [[taoism]]'s water-wears-down-stone, though as convergence rather than lineage.

## Hospitality and the secret

- *Of Hospitality* (1997, with Jacques Derrida — her invitation essay runs alongside his seminars): unconditional welcome versus the conditions every actual welcome imposes; the stranger at the threshold as the test of ethics. Direct purchase on immigration work, shelter work, and who gets welcomed on what terms.
- She also defended the secret — the right to interiority, slowness, and the unshared — against the culture of mandatory transparency. For clinicians: confidentiality isn't merely a rule; it's the protected dark where change can happen.

## Open questions

- How does her account of risk sit with clinical risk-assessment culture, which is institutionally zero-risk? Is there a practice-honest synthesis?
- Is *douceur* sustainable as a professional stance, or does it require conditions most institutions don't provide? (This is a burnout question in disguise.)

## Related Media

- **Derrida** (2002, documentary) — her collaborator and milieu; hospitality thought out loud on camera.
- **The Gleaners and I** (Agnès Varda, 2000) — gentleness as documentary method: sustained attention to what everyone else discards.
- **Free Solo** (2018) — watch it as philosophy: what total risk does to a life, and what refusing it would have cost. She would have had notes.
- **Pierre Bonnard's interiors** — paintings where domestic tenderness is the entire subject; *douceur* in pigment.
