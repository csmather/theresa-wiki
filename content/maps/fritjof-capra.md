Austrian-born physicist (b. 1939, Vienna) who left particle physics to spend fifty years arguing one idea from different directions: reality — especially living reality — is a network of relationships, not a pile of parts. He's the most readable bridge between hard science and the systems thinking that social work quietly runs on.

## The Tao of Physics (1975)

- The claim: twentieth-century physics (quantum theory, relativity) independently arrived at pictures long held by Eastern traditions — interconnection, process over substance, the observer entangled with the observed. Written as a set of parallels between the laboratory and [[taoism|the Tao]].
- Enormous popular success; working physicists mostly rolled their eyes ("quantum mysticism"). The fair version of the criticism: the physics analogies are looser than the confident prose implies.
- Still worth knowing as a cultural event — it licensed a generation to connect science with meaning, and it's the door through which Capra reached his sturdier later material.

## The systems view of life

- *The Web of Life* (1996) and *The Systems View of Life* (2014, with Pier Luigi Luisi) synthesize the systems account of biology: living systems are self-organizing networks that continuously remake themselves (Maturana and Varela's *autopoiesis*).
- Santiago theory of cognition: cognition is the process of life itself — mind is not a thing but the activity of a living network. Even a cell "knows" in this thin but rigorous sense.
- The shift he keeps pointing at, in every book: from objects to relationships, from quantities to qualities, from hierarchies to networks, from structure to process.

## Why social work should care

- Person-in-environment — the discipline's core stance — *is* systems theory. Bronfenbrenner's nested ecological systems, family systems therapy, community practice: all assume you can't understand or help an individual apart from the web they're embedded in, because the individual largely is their relations.
- Capra supplies a natural-science backbone for that instinct: it isn't soft-headedness; it's how life actually organizes.
- The same intuition on the design side: [[christopher-alexander]] on living structure in buildings and neighborhoods.

## Open questions

- Where exactly is the line between the durable systems science and the new-age packaging? Working rule so far: keep the networks, hold the quantum loosely.
- Did general systems theory (Bertalanffy) flow directly into social work's ecological model, or did they arrive in parallel?

## Related Media

- **Mindwalk** (1990) — a feature film of Capra's ideas, directed by his brother Bernt: a physicist, a poet, and a politician walk around Mont-Saint-Michel arguing about systems. Slow in a good way.
- **Powers of Ten** (Eames Office, 1977, 9 min) — scale and nestedness in one continuous zoom.
- **Koyaanisqatsi** (1982) — systems out of balance; no dialogue required.
- **An Ecology of Mind** (2010) — Nora Bateson's portrait of her father Gregory Bateson, the deeper and stranger systems thinker behind much of this lineage.
