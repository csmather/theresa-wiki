Chilean psychoanalyst (1908–1995; trained in London, taught in Santiago, spent his later career in Rome) who made the strangest and possibly most useful move in post-Freudian theory: he treated [[sigmund-freud|Freud]]'s unconscious not as a place or a story but as a different *logic* — and then worked out its rules.

## Bi-logic

- Ordinary conscious thought runs on *asymmetric* logic: relations have direction. "Maria is my mother" is not the same as "I am Maria's mother." Before is not after. The part is not the whole.
- The unconscious adds a second logic. **Principle of symmetry**: the unconscious treats every relation as reversible — if A relates to B, B relates identically to A. **Principle of generalization**: an individual thing gets treated as its entire class.
- Run those two rules and Freud's famous "characteristics of the unconscious" fall out as consequences instead of curiosities: timelessness (before = after), displacement (any member of the class will do), condensation (part = whole), tolerance of contradiction. Freud described the phenomena; Matte Blanco derived them.
- The cleanest teaching example (from [[clarke-nature-of-bilogic|Clarke's exposition]]): you dream your friend Peter turns into a dragon. Read as a derivation — *Peter is dangerous; dragons are dangerous things; dangerous things are dragons; therefore Peter is a dragon.* Step three reverses a sub-class relation, which ordinary logic forbids — and the unconscious performs routinely. Dreams aren't loose "associations"; they're valid proofs in the other logic.

## The unconscious as infinite sets

- His 1975 magnum opus (*The Unconscious as Infinite Sets: An Essay in Bi-Logic*) borrows from mathematics: infinite sets are precisely the sets where a part can equal the whole — so symmetric logic is the logic of infinity, and strong emotion always carries that infinite quality (adored, catastrophic, forever).
- The mind is stratified: surface levels are mostly asymmetric (reasoning, planning), deeper strata increasingly symmetric, until at the deepest level everything is everything else.

## Clinical cash value

- When a client says "everything is ruined," "everyone leaves," "it will always be like this" — that's not just cognitive distortion, it's symmetric logic surfacing: one loss has become the infinite class of all losses.
- The therapeutic move is translation between the logics: honoring that the feeling genuinely is infinite while lending asymmetric structure back to it ("this happened, then, with that person").
- The larger gift: a way to take affect seriously as *structured* rather than as noise interrupting thought. Panic, mania, dreams, and falling in love become different mixtures of the same two logics.

## Afterlife: taking the math seriously

- Matte Blanco attracted a small but serious formalist following. [[clarke-nature-of-bilogic|Chris Clarke]] (a mathematician) reconstructs bi-logic as a rigorous "context-dependent logic" and places it in the family of non-classical logics alongside quantum logic and intuitionism.
- The refinements matter less than the verdict: the system survives formal scrutiny as a *descriptive* model of how thought is generated — not a broken logic but a different one, statable with full precision.

## Open questions

- How does bi-logic sit next to CBT's "cognitive distortions" list — rival explanation, or the deeper grammar underneath it?
- Does symmetric logic correspond to anything in modern affective neuroscience, or is it purely descriptive?
