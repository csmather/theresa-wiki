Founder of [[psychoanalysis]] (1856–1939). This map skips most of the biography and keeps what a clinician-in-training actually inherits: a working model of a mind that is not transparent to itself, and a method built on that assumption.

## The model of the mind

- **The dynamic unconscious** — not just "things you're not currently thinking about," but material actively kept out of awareness (repression) that keeps expressing itself sideways: dreams, slips, symptoms, patterns you repeat while swearing you won't.
- Two kinds of thinking: rational, ordered *secondary process* sits on top of *primary process* — image-driven, timeless, contradiction-tolerant. ([[ignacio-matte-blanco]] later worked out primary process as a logic of its own.)
- The models evolved: topographic (unconscious / preconscious / conscious) → structural (id / ego / superego). Later and darker: *Beyond the Pleasure Principle* (1920) introduces repetition compulsion and the death drive — the observation that people reliably repeat what hurts them. This late Freud is the one [[slavoj-zizek]] and [[alenka-zupancic]] build on.

## The origin story, in his own words

- The [[freud-five-lectures-clark|Five Lectures]] (Clark University, 1909 — his only American appearance) remain the best compact introduction: Freud narrating the discovery himself.
- It starts with a patient he never treated: Josef Breuer's "Anna O.," whose symptoms dissolved when their forgotten origins were spoken aloud — the "talking cure" was her phrase. Freud's summary line: "hysterics suffer mainly from reminiscences."
- The pivotal observation: patients *resist* remembering exactly what most needs remembering — and that resistance is the fingerprint of the force (repression) that buried the material. The obstacle is the map.
- The lectures end not with liberation-of-desire but with **sublimation**: redirecting drive energy toward work, art, and connection. Freud was never the libertine his critics imagined.

## Clinical inventions that stuck

- **Free association** — say whatever comes, unedited; the places where saying becomes hard are the map.
- **Dreams** treated as meaningful, interpretable productions — "the royal road to the unconscious."
- **Transference** — clients re-live old relational templates with the clinician. Instead of a contaminant, it's the material. **Counter-transference**: the clinician's own stirred-up response, also information.
- **Defense mechanisms** (systematized by his daughter Anna Freud) — repression, projection, denial, sublimation... vocabulary so absorbed into everyday English that people forget it was ever technical.

## What survives, what doesn't

- Weak by modern evidential standards: psychosexual stages as literal child development, the specifics of dream symbolism, most of the energetic metapsychology.
- Durable: the dynamic unconscious (echoed in modern implicit-process research), transference, defenses, and the talking cure itself — every therapy that trades on the relationship descends from this consulting room.
- Necessary critiques to carry alongside: patriarchal assumptions (the Dora case is the classic example), pathologizing of women and homosexuality, universalizing from a narrow slice of Viennese patients. Reading Freud well means reading him with the case against him in hand.
- Social work history runs straight through him: American casework absorbed psychoanalysis wholesale in the 1920s–40s (the "psychiatric deluge"); psychodynamic listening, "use of self," and supervision culture are the residue.

## Open questions

- Which parts of the MSW curriculum are quietly Freudian without citation? (Running list: trauma repetition, therapeutic alliance, defense-informed assessment...)
- The relational/attachment turn (Bowlby, Winnicott, relational psychoanalysis) deserves its own map when sources land — it's the bridge between classical Freud and current practice.

## Related Media

- **The Century of the Self** (Adam Curtis, 2002) — how Freud's ideas, via his nephew Edward Bernays, built public relations and consumer culture. Essential and unsettling viewing for anyone entering a helping profession.
- **A Dangerous Method** (David Cronenberg, 2011) — Freud, Jung, and Sabina Spielrein; the founding triangle, dramatized.
- **Spellbound** (Hitchcock, 1945) — Hollywood falls in love with psychoanalysis, complete with a dream sequence designed by Salvador Dalí.
- **Louise Bourgeois' sculpture** (*Maman*, the *Cells*) — psychoanalysis worked in bronze and fabric; she spent decades in analysis and made art of it deliberately.
