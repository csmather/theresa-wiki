Slovenian philosopher (b. 1949), the loudest export of the Ljubljana school ([[alenka-zupancic]], Mladen Dolar). Under the jokes, the machinery is consistent: Lacan's psychoanalysis plus Hegel plus Marx, aimed at one question — why do we keep choosing what harms us, individually and collectively?

## Ideology, upgraded

- The old model: ideology is false belief, so show people the truth and they'll stop. Žižek's diagnosis: ideology today runs on *cynical reason* — "they know very well what they are doing, and they are doing it anyway." Knowing better changes nothing, because—
- Ideology lives in practice and fantasy, not opinions: in what we *do* (rituals, purchases, small daily compliances) and in the fantasies organizing our desire underneath whatever beliefs we'd disavow at dinner.
- Signature teaching examples: canned laughter (the television enjoys on your behalf); the sunglasses in *They Live* — with the twist that seeing ideology is painful, which is why the film needs a six-minute fistfight to get them on.
- *The Sublime Object of Ideology* (1989) is the founding book: symptom and enjoyment (jouissance) as political categories. We are libidinally attached to our chains — which is why critique alone frees no one. Same conceptual toolbox as [[alenka-zupancic|Zupančič]]'s work on the Real.

## Why a social worker might care

- Structural social work needs an account of why people defend systems that grind them down. Cynical reason and libidinal investment are that account, and they're sharper than "false consciousness."
- His critique of charity — consumption with redemption folded into the price (buy the coffee, save the rainforest) — is a sharp lens on the nonprofit-industrial complex. The RSA talk below is the ten-minute version.
- The general lesson for practice: don't expect information to do the work of transformation. [[sigmund-freud|Freud]] said the same thing about interpretation — the problem is never just ignorance, it's attachment.

## Handle with care

- Method: joke → Hegel → Hitchcock, repeat. Brilliant and repetitive; the books openly remix each other.
- Deliberately provocative, sometimes past usefulness; his political positions are contested from every direction, often simultaneously. Read him as a lens-grinder, not an oracle.

## Open questions

- Is there a practice-level use of ideology critique — in supervision, community organizing, policy work — or is it strictly diagnostic?
- Where exactly does his account of enjoyment-as-attachment meet repetition compulsion? (Suspicion: they're the same observation at different scales.)
