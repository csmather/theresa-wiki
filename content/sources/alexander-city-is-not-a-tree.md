## Summary

Christopher Alexander's 1965 essay (originally in *Architectural Forum*, two parts) — his most famous short work and the conceptual seed of everything after. The "tree" in the title is the mathematical structure, not the plant: a hierarchy in which subsets nest neatly and never overlap. Alexander's argument is that cities that grew naturally over time ("natural cities" — Siena, Kyoto, Manhattan) have the structure of a *semilattice* — units overlap and cross-connect — while professionally planned cities ("artificial cities" — Brasília, Chandigarh, the British New Towns, Levittown) are designed as trees. The missing overlap is why planned cities feel dead. The human mind reaches for trees because they're easier to hold, but the resulting simplification amputates the city's living structure.

## Key Claims

- A city's structure is defined by which sets of elements actually function as units; in living cities these units overlap (a semilattice); in planned cities they're forced into non-overlapping nested hierarchies (a tree).
- A tree containing 20 elements can hold at most 19 subsets, while a semilattice on the same 20 elements can hold over a million — the tree is not just a simplification but a drastic structural impoverishment.
- Concrete overlap example: a corner drugstore, the newsrack, the traffic light, and the pedestrians waiting to cross form a functioning urban unit that cuts across any zoning category.
- Planners' separations — home/work, campus/city, play zones designated for children — sever real interdependencies; life happens in the overlaps (children actually play in streets, alleys, everywhere the plan didn't designate).
- The preference for trees is cognitive, not malicious: designers can't hold semilattice complexity in one mind, so they trim it. The remedy is humility toward grown structure, not cleverer master plans.
