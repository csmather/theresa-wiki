## Summary

Essay by Fritjof Capra published by the Center for Ecoliteracy (which he co-founded), adapted from a talk on linking food, health, and environment. A compact statement of his mature position: today's global crises (food insecurity, climate, resource depletion) are systemic and interconnected, and addressing them requires ecological literacy — understanding how nature actually sustains life — plus the perceptual shifts of systems thinking.

## Key Claims

- Life's defining characteristic is metabolism: "the ceaseless flow of energy and matter through a network of chemical reactions" by which living systems generate and perpetuate themselves.
- Nature sustains life "by creating and nurturing communities" — no organism exists in isolation.
- Five ecological principles: waste cycles through food webs; matter circulates continuously; solar energy drives the cycles; diversity ensures resilience; life spreads by networking, not combat.
- Systems thinking as a set of perceptual shifts: from parts to wholes ("the whole is more than the sum of its parts"), from objects to relationships, from measuring to mapping (relationships can't be fully quantified), from structure to process.
- World problems are interlocked — e.g., oil dependence ties food prices to fuel, producing perverse outcomes like grain feeding vehicles instead of people — so single-issue fixes fail; the crises share a root in ignoring ecological patterns.
