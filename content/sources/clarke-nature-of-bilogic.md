## Summary

Draft paper by Chris Clarke (Faculty of Mathematical Studies, University of Southampton): "On the nature of bilogic: the work of Ignacio Matte Blanco." A mathematician's careful exposition and partial reconstruction of Matte Blanco's system, drawing on Rodney Bomford's reformulation. Clarke's aim is to give bi-logic a rigorous formulation — a "context dependent logic" — and place it in the family of non-classical logics (alongside quantum logic and intuitionism), while flagging where Matte Blanco's own derivations don't quite hold.

The paper's teaching example is the clearest short statement of the whole theory: in a dream, a friend (Peter) turns into a dragon. Instead of loose "association," Matte Blanco reads the dream as a quasi-logical derivation — *Peter is dangerous; dragons are dangerous things; dangerous things are dragons; therefore Peter is a dragon.* The step "dangerous things are dragons" is a non sequitur in ordinary logic (sub-class relations don't reverse), and that is exactly the point: the unconscious treats all relations as symmetric.

## Key Claims

- Matte Blanco's central postulate: the Unconscious treats *all* relations as symmetric; from this single postulate a wide range of unconscious phenomena follows in a unified way.
- Bi-logic is the interplay of this symmetric logic with ordinary asymmetric logic — most actual thinking is a mixture of the two.
- Clarke (following Bomford) separates the two founding principles — symmetry (logic) and generalization (set theory) — and argues generalization shouldn't be *derived* from symmetry but grounded in a reformulated logic.
- Infinity is better handled through the concept of *order* than Matte Blanco's cardinality-based treatment, which Clarke thinks is truer to the psychological data.
- Bi-logic is not a logic in the prescriptive sense (its conclusions are "incorrect"), but works as a *descriptive* model of the generative processes underlying thought — and can be made mathematically rigorous as a context-dependent logic.
- Matte Blanco distinguishes the (Freudian) Unconscious as a specific system from mere "unconsciousness"; consciousness for him is focal, propositional, reflexive ("cannot focus on more than one thing at a time"), while "the experience of being cannot be described."
