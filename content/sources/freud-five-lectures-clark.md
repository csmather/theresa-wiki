## Summary

Freud's only American lectures, delivered at Clark University in 1909 and published as "The Origin and Development of Psychoanalysis" (1910). Five short talks in which Freud himself tells the origin story of psychoanalysis for a general educated audience — still the best compact introduction in his own voice. He begins with Josef Breuer's patient "Anna O." and the discovery that hysterical symptoms dissolved when their forgotten origins were spoken (the "talking cure"), then walks through how he replaced hypnosis with free association, what resistance in the consulting room revealed about repression, how dreams and slips betray the unconscious in everyone (not just patients), the theory of infantile sexuality, and finally transference and sublimation.

## Key Claims

- Hysterical symptoms are not random malfunctions: they are "residues and memory symbols" of forgotten emotional experiences — "hysterics suffer mainly from reminiscences."
- Symptoms can be dissolved by bringing the pathogenic memory, with its strangled affect, back into speech — the cathartic method discovered with Anna O.
- The difficulty patients have in recalling these memories (resistance) is the clinician's clue: the same force that resists remembering is the force that repressed the memory in the first place.
- Repression arises from conflict between a wish and the ego's standards; the repressed wish doesn't vanish but expresses itself in disguised substitutes (symptoms).
- Free association replaces hypnosis: nothing that occurs to the patient is arbitrary; interpretation can recover the repressed from what the patient says, dreams, and does.
- Dreams are the "royal road" to the unconscious; slips and everyday errors show the same mechanisms operating in healthy people — psychoanalysis is a general psychology, not just a theory of illness.
- Sexuality (broadly conceived) begins in childhood; symptoms regularly trace back to conflicts over sexual wishes.
- Transference — the patient re-directing old feelings onto the physician — appears spontaneously and becomes the engine of treatment.
- Not all liberated desire should be "acted out": sublimation redirects drive energy toward higher aims; civilization runs on this exchange.
