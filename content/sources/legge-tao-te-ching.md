## Summary

James Legge's complete scholarly translation of the Tao Te Ching (81 chapters), published in 1891 as part of his *Texts of Taoism* in the Sacred Books of the East series — the great Victorian sinologist's rendering, now public domain. Formal and heavily annotated where modern versions are loose and lyrical; reading it against a modern rendition shows exactly what each translator chose. All the canonical furniture is here: the unnameable tao (ch. 1), the usefulness of emptiness — thirty spokes, one hub; the vessel's hollow; the room's space (ch. 11), water as the emblem of the soft overcoming the hard (ch. 78), wu wei as doing-without-striving throughout, and governing a great state "as we cook small fish" (ch. 60).

## Key Claims

- "The Tao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Tao. The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name." (ch. 1)
- Usefulness depends on what is not there: the empty hub makes the wheel, the hollow makes the vessel, the door and windows make the room. (ch. 11)
- "There is nothing in the world more soft and weak than water, and yet for attacking things that are firm and strong there is nothing that can take precedence of it." (ch. 78)
- The sage "acts without doing" — accomplishing without striving, leading without mastering; the best rulers are barely known to exist. (chs. 2, 17, 63)
- Opposites define each other — being and non-being, difficult and easy, high and low "give birth" to one another. (ch. 2)
- Victorian frame worth noticing: Legge reads the text as a classic for scholars and statesmen — precisely the register modern renditions like Le Guin's deliberately strip away.
