## Summary

Le Guin's six-minute acceptance speech for the National Book Foundation's Medal for Distinguished Contribution to American Letters, November 2014 — delivered to a hall full of publishers and instantly famous. She shares the award with her fellow "writers of the imagination" — the science fiction and fantasy writers excluded from literature for fifty years — then turns to the coming hard times, when we will need "writers who can remember freedom": realists of a larger reality. The back half is a direct attack on the commodification of publishing, culminating in the most quoted thing she ever said.

## Key Claims

- Hard times are coming: we'll want writers who can see alternatives to how we live now, who can imagine real grounds for hope — "poets, visionaries — realists of a larger reality."
- Imaginative fiction was excluded from "literature" for decades while it did exactly this work; she accepts the award on its behalf.
- Books are not commodities: "the profit motive is often in conflict with the aims of art."
- The line: "We live in capitalism. Its power seems inescapable. So did the divine right of kings. Any human power can be resisted and changed by human beings."
- Where change starts: "Resistance and change often begin in art, and very often in our art — the art of words."
- Writers should demand their fair share of the proceeds but never let profit define what gets written or how.
