## Summary

Text of Slavoj Žižek's *How to Read Lacan* (Granta, 2006) as hosted at lacan.com. Žižek's most accessible book: each chapter takes one Lacanian concept and works it through examples from film, religion, politics, and everyday ritual. It doubles as the friendliest serious introduction to Lacan available — Lacan explained by his loudest reader — covering the big Other, empty gestures and performatives, the interpassive subject, jouissance and the superego injunction to enjoy, fantasy and the question *Che vuoi?* ("what do you want from me?"), and the Real.

## Key Claims

- Lacan's "return to Freud": the unconscious is not a cauldron of instincts but is "structured like a language" — it thinks, it speaks, and it is out in the open (in slips, jokes, symptoms), not buried in depths.
- The **big Other**: the symbolic order — the invisible register of rules, appearances, and expectations we all behave *as if* someone were monitoring. It doesn't exist as a thing, yet it organizes social life; much of politeness, ideology, and institutional behavior is performed for it.
- The **empty gesture**: the offer made to be refused (the "you really don't have to..." that must be declined). Symbolic exchange runs on such gestures; freedom of choice is often the obligation to make the right choice while pretending it was free.
- **Interpassivity**: the object can enjoy *for* us — canned laughter laughs on your behalf, the Tibetan prayer wheel prays for you, the mourner hired to weep grieves for you. Even our most intimate feelings can be outsourced.
- The **superego** is not simply the voice of prohibition; its deepest command today is "Enjoy!" — an injunction more tyrannical than any ban, because you can always fail at it.
- **Fantasy** is not the opposite of reality: it teaches us how to desire and thereby structures reality itself; the anxious question underneath is *Che vuoi?* — what does the Other want from me?
- Method demonstration throughout: high theory read through low culture (Hitchcock, ads, jokes), on the principle that ideology lives in the everyday.
