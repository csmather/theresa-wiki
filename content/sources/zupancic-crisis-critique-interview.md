## Summary

Long interview with Alenka Zupančič by Agon Hamza and Frank Ruda, *Crisis and Critique* vol. 6, issue 1 (March 2019). The best single tour of her project in her own words: why philosophy needs psychoanalysis, what *What Is Sex?* actually claims, her account of sexual difference against identity-politics framings, a reversal of Althusser on ideology, and closing reflections on #MeToo, political passion, and the "obscenity of power."

## Key Claims

- Psychoanalysis matters to philosophy because it touches the core questions "What is a subject?" and "What is an object?" — Lacan showed that notions philosophy wanted to abandon (subject, truth, the real) shouldn't be dropped but thought *with their inherent contradiction*.
- Against the theory/clinic split: psychoanalytic concepts are not derived from the clinic like lab results; they "comprise" the clinical element in themselves. Working with them productively requires "an ear... for that bit of the real" — which not every theorist *or* every practicing analyst has.
- Psychoanalysis is not a science in the usual sense because it insists on a dimension of truth irreducible to accuracy — but that calls for a different kind of formalization, not a retreat from rigor.
- *What Is Sex?* is a claim, not a question: we talk about sex as if we knew what it is, and we don't. Sexuality is inherently "impossible" — problematic from within, not just because of external prohibition — and Freud discovered this negative core, not sexuality itself.
- Sexuality is the point of "short circuit between ontology and epistemology": the limit of what we can know may tell us something about a "lapse of being" itself. The unconscious is not sexual because of dirty contents but because of *how it works* (censorship, substitution, condensation are themselves "sexual" operations).
- Sexual difference is not a difference between gender identities; it's an impossibility inherent to the discursive order that identity-formation keeps pushing outward (visible as the "+" in LGBTQ+). Recognized identity by itself protects no one — "woman" was always a recognized position and discrimination proceeded through its very definitions.
- Reversal of Althusser: not "ideology interpellates individuals into subjects" but "ideology interpellates subjects into individuals with this or that identity." The subject-as-crack is both what gives ideology its grip and its inner breaking point.
- On #MeToo: significant precisely as a movement; its danger is becoming a club of victims petitioning the Other for protection — the vital move is assuming power to create social change, "leaving behind the identity of victimhood," connected to broader social solidarity.
- Political passion is not a private feeling let into politics in doses; politics *is* articulated communal passion, and decades of cleansing passion from public space fed today's populisms (passions in search of a Leader, not a politics).
- "Obscenity of power": openly displayed faults and appetites disarm critique because nothing is hidden — but the spectacle of "I can afford it" has a short breath.
